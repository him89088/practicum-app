Introduction to the Research Study:


Topic: Effects of Chronic Pain on Human Decision Making by using a Reinforcement Learning model
School: School of Computing & School of Psychology
Principal Investigators: Himanshu Vashisht (himanshu.vashisht2@mail.dcu.ie) & Alekhya Nethra (alekhya.nethra2@mail.dcu.ie)
Other Investigators: Prof. Tomas Ward, Lili Zhang, Dr. Brian Slattery

Research Summary:


According to chronicpain.ie, chronic pain is a disease of the central nervous system. Chronic pain may originate in the body, or in the brain or spinal cord. It is often difficult to treat. Approximately 20% of the world’s population suffers from ongoing pain that severely impacts their quality of life.

There is a growth in how computational methods such as reinforcement learning (RL), where the aim of the agent (human or computer) is to maximise the rewards, could be useful in research. In such experiments, the researchers use a number of economic games such as Iowa Gambling task (IGT), Wisconsin Card Sorting Task (WCST), and Stroop Task (ST), to study the effects of some form of stimuli (chronic pain, in this case) in the player’s performance.

We aim to follow the same principle and study how chronic pain affects human decision (i.e. a player’s performance) by making our subjects play a number of rounds of few of these games and capture the performance and analysing it.

If you agree to participate in this project:


First you will be asked to answer some questions regarding your experience of chronic pain. Following this, you are required to play a specified number of rounds of few games, some via web application. In total, you should be able to complete the all tasks in approximately 20 minutes.

No personal information is collected:


We identify you by an ID number we randomly assign you.
We do not ask for your:
  • Name
  • E-Mail
  • Phone Number
We do ask for your:
  • Gender
  • Age-range (for example 26 – 30, or 31 – 35)
  • Pain level while performing the task (on a scale of 1-10, 1 being lowest and 10 being highest)

Anticipated benefits from participating in this project:


The indirect benefits will arise from an increase in the understanding of the cognitive impact of chronic pain which will be disseminated in a scientific article. We do not foresee any risks to participants from completing these tasks. Participants will be free to change their mind and withdraw from the study at any time until they submit their final response and tick to consent to their responses being collected.

Since no personal data is collected, participants will not be informed of the results of this project. However, the completed work will be submitted for publication where the anonymised results will be displayed.
